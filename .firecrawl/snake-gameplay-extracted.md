# Snake — Gameplay & Mechanics (extracted)

## Core concept

Snake is a genre of action video games where the player maneuvers the end of a growing line, often themed as a snake. The player must keep the snake from colliding with both other obstacles and itself, which gets harder as the snake lengthens.

The concept evolved into a single-player variant where a line with a head and tail gets longer with each piece of food eaten — often apples or eggs — increasing the likelihood of self-collision.

## Gameplay

A single-player game where both the head and tail move, and each item eaten makes the snake longer.

The original genre format is a two-player game. Viewed from a top-down perspective, each player controls a "snake" with a fixed starting position. The "head" of the snake continually moves forward, unable to stop, growing ever longer. It must be steered left, right, up, and down to avoid hitting walls and the body of either snake. The player who survives the longest wins.

Single-player versions are less prevalent and have one or more snakes controlled by the computer.

In the most common single-player game, the player's snake is of a certain length, so when the head moves the tail does too. Each item eaten by the snake causes the snake to get longer. In some variants the snake eats apples; in others, the snake eats abstract objects in a maze.

## Mechanic variations

- The snake may be controlled using the left and right arrow keys relative to the direction it is heading in (turn-relative input), rather than absolute up/down/left/right.
- The snake may increase in speed as it gets longer.
- Some versions give the player only one life.
- In some versions the snake fits tightly into a maze, and the gameplay is faster than most snake designs.
- Variants may add enemy snakes to the familiar food-eating gameplay.

## Visuals / presentation

- Top-down perspective.
- Originally purely abstract visuals — no snake imagery — just a growing line on the playfield.
- Later themes include snakes eating apples, snakes navigating mazes, and light-cycle-style trails.

## Why it endures (design notes)

- The genre requires both quick reactions and forethought — the player must react to immediate obstacles while planning a path that won't trap the lengthening tail.
- Simplicity and low technical requirements have resulted in hundreds of versions.
