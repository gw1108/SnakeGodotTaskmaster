# Snake (video game genre)

Snake (video game genre)

Video game genre

This article is about the video game genre. For the Nokia phone game in that genre, see [Snake (1998 video game)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(1998_video_game) "Snake (1998 video game)").

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/0/04/Snake_trs-80.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Snake_trs-80.jpg) _Snake_ on a TRS-80

**Snake** is a [genre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Video_game_genre "Video game genre") of [action video games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_game "Action game") where the player maneuvers the end of a growing line, often themed as a [snake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake "Snake"). The player must keep the snake from colliding with both other obstacles and itself, which gets harder as the snake lengthens.

The genre originated in the 1976 competitive [arcade video game](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arcade_video_game "Arcade video game") _[Blockade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blockade_(video_game) "Blockade (video game)")_ from [Gremlin Industries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gremlin_Industries "Gremlin Industries") where the goal is to survive longer than the other player does. _Blockade_ and the initial wave of clones that followed were purely abstract and did not use _snake_ terminology. The concept evolved into a single-player variant where a line with a head and tail gets longer with each piece of food eaten—often apples or eggs—increasing the likelihood of self-collision. The simplicity and low technical requirements of snake games have resulted in hundreds of versions, some of which have the word _snake_ or _worm_ in the title. The 1982 _[Tron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tron_(video_game) "Tron (video game)")_ arcade video game, based on the film, includes snake gameplay for the single-player [Light Cycles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_Cycle "Light Cycle") segment, and some later snake games borrow the theme.

After a version simply called _[Snake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(1998_video_game) "Snake (1998 video game)")_ was preloaded on [Nokia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nokia "Nokia") [mobile phones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mobile_phone "Mobile phone") in 1998, there was a resurgence of interest in snake games.

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/55/Snake_can_be_completed.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Snake_can_be_completed.gif) A single-player game, where both the head and tail move, and each item eaten makes the snake longer

The original _[Blockade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blockade_(video_game) "Blockade (video game)")_ from 1976 and its many clones are two-player games. Viewed from a top-down perspective, each player controls a "snake" with a fixed starting position. The "head" of the snake continually moves forward, unable to stop, growing ever longer. It must be steered left, right, up, and down to avoid hitting walls and the body of either snake. The player who survives the longest wins.[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-1) Single-player versions are less prevalent and have one or more snakes controlled by the computer, as in the light cycles segment of the 1982 _[Tron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tron_(video_game) "Tron (video game)")_ arcade game.\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]

In the most common single-player game, the player's snake is of a certain length, so when the head moves the tail does too. Each item eaten by the snake causes the snake to get longer. _[Snake Byte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_Byte "Snake Byte")_ has the snake eating apples. _[Nibbler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nibbler_(video_game) "Nibbler (video game)")_ has the snake eating abstract objects in a maze.\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]

## History

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Cgasnake.png/500px-Cgasnake.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cgasnake.png) _Snake_ on an IBM PC rendered in a text mode[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Matopeli_telmac_1800.JPG/500px-Matopeli_telmac_1800.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Matopeli_telmac_1800.JPG) _Snake_ on a [Telmac 1800](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telmac_1800 "Telmac 1800"), [CHIP-8](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CHIP-8 "CHIP-8"), published 1978[\[2\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-2)

The Snake genre began with the 1976 [arcade video game](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arcade_video_game "Arcade video game") _[Blockade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blockade_(video_game) "Blockade (video game)")_[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-Goggin-3)[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-DeMaria-4) written by Lane Hauck and Ago Kiss[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-giantlist-5) and published by [Gremlin Industries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gremlin_Industries "Gremlin Industries").[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-6) It was cloned as _Bigfoot Bonkers_ the same year. In 1977, [Atari, Inc.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atari,_Inc. "Atari, Inc.") released two _Blockade_-inspired games: the arcade game _[Dominos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dominos_(video_game) "Dominos (video game)")_ and Atari VCS game _[Surround](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surround_(video_game) "Surround (video game)")_.[\[7\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-KLOV-7) _Surround_ was one of the nine Atari VCS launch titles in the US and was sold by [Sears](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sears "Sears") under the name _Chase_. That same year, a similar game was launched for the [Bally Astrocade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bally_Astrocade "Bally Astrocade") as _Checkmate_.[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-8) Mattel released _[Snafu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snafu_(video_game) "Snafu (video game)")_ for the [Intellivision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intellivision "Intellivision") console in 1982.

The first known [home computer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home_computer "Home computer") version, _Worm_, was programmed by [Peter Trefonas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Trefonas "Peter Trefonas") for the [TRS-80](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TRS-80 "TRS-80") and published by _CLOAD_ magazine in 1978.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-Goggin-3) Versions followed from the same author for the [PET](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commodore_PET "Commodore PET") and [Apple II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_II "Apple II"). An authorized version of the _Hustle_ arcade game, itself a clone of _Blockade_, was published by [Milton Bradley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milton_Bradley_Company "Milton Bradley Company") for the [TI-99/4A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TI-99/4A "TI-99/4A") in 1980.[\[9\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-9)

The single-player _[Snake Byte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_Byte "Snake Byte")_ was published in 1982 for Atari 8-bit computers, Apple II, and VIC-20; a snake eats apples to complete a level, growing longer in the process. In _Snake_ for the [BBC Micro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BBC_Micro "BBC Micro") (1982), by Dave Bresnen, the snake is controlled using the left and right arrow keys relative to the direction it is heading in. The snake increases in speed as it gets longer, and there is only one life.

_[Nibbler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nibbler_(video_game) "Nibbler (video game)")_ (1982) is a single-player arcade game where the snake fits tightly into a maze, and the gameplay is faster than most snake designs. Another single-player version is part of the 1982 _[Tron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tron_(video_game) "Tron (video game)")_ arcade game, themed with light cycles. It reinvigorated the snake concept, and many subsequent games borrowed the light cycle theme.

Starting in 1991, _[Nibbles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nibbles_(video_game) "Nibbles (video game)")_ was included with [MS-DOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MS-DOS "MS-DOS") for a period of time as a [QBasic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/QBasic "QBasic") sample program. In 1992, _[Rattler Race](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rattler_Race "Rattler Race")_ was released as part of the second _[Microsoft Entertainment Pack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft_Entertainment_Pack "Microsoft Entertainment Pack")_. It adds enemy snakes to the familiar apple-eating gameplay.

In 1998, the [mobile game](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mobile_game "Mobile game") _[Snake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(1998_video_game) "Snake (1998 video game)")_ was released for [Nokia 6110](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nokia_6110 "Nokia 6110").[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-10) The game was popular, and [Nokia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nokia "Nokia") released a series of reiterations, including _[Snake II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_II "Snake II")_, _Snake EX_, _Snake EX2_, _Snake III_, _[Snakes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snakes_(N-Gage_game) "Snakes (N-Gage game)")_, _Snake Xenzia_ and _[Snakes Subsonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snakes_Subsonic "Snakes Subsonic")_. As the game graphics and gameplay evolved, it became less popular.[\[11\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-11)

A series of online _Snake_ games were made. Google has incorporated Snake games into its applications. In 2010, [YouTube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/YouTube "YouTube") added _Snake_ as a hidden game inside of their [video player](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Video_player "Video player").[\[32\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-32) In 2013, [Google](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google "Google") launched _Snake_ [doodle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google_Doodle "Google Doodle") as an [easter egg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Easter_egg_(media) "Easter egg (media)") for [web browsers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_browser "Web browser").[\[33\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-33) In 2019, Google added _Snake_ inside [Google Maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google_Maps "Google Maps") as an [April Fools' Day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/April_Fools'_Day "April Fools' Day") prank.[\[34\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-34) In 2019, [Google Chrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google_Chrome "Google Chrome") launched _Snake Game_ for web browsers.[\[35\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-35) Google relaunched _Doodle Snake_ in January 2025 to celebrate the Year of the [Snake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(zodiac) "Snake (zodiac)") of the [Chinese New Year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_New_Year "Chinese New Year").[\[36\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-36)

In 2025, the concept of _Snake_ was adapted into an educational context. The open-source game _Snakeleev_[\[37\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-37) uses the established gameplay mechanics to help students learn the [periodic table of elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodic_table "Periodic table"). The game was developed as part of a science education project and is described in a peer-reviewed article published in the _[Journal of Chemical Education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Journal_of_Chemical_Education "Journal of Chemical Education")_.[\[38\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-38) A 2025 season of the travel competition series _[Jet Lag: The Game](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jet_Lag:_The_Game "Jet Lag: The Game")_, played in South Korea, was based on the game design.[\[39\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-39) _Python Snake_ is often used to teach students how to program in [Python](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Python_(programming_language) "Python (programming language)") and [Pygame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pygame "Pygame").[\[40\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-40)[\[41\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-41)

## Legacy

In 1996, _[Next Generation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Next_Generation_(magazine) "Next Generation (magazine)")_ ranked it number 41 on their "Top 100 Games of All Time", citing the need for both quick reactions and forethought. In lieu of a title for a specific version, they listed it as "Snake game" in quotes.[\[42\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-42)

On November 29, 2012, the [Museum of Modern Art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art "Museum of Modern Art") in New York City announced that the Nokia port of Snake was one of 40 games that the curators wished to add to the museum's collection in the future.[\[43\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snake_(video_game_genre)#cite_note-43)
